Plasmonic Metasurfaces

My research on plasmonic metasurfaces focuses on developing functional optical interfaces using scalable and bottom up nanofabrication strategies. A central goal is to design nanoparticle assemblies that support collective plasmonic responses rather than isolated particle resonances. These systems allow precise control over electromagnetic field localization, which is critical for spectroscopy and sensing. I have worked extensively on organizing plasmonic nanoparticles into ordered or quasi ordered arrangements using template assisted and colloidal assembly routes. This enables reproducible hot spot formation and improves signal stability in surface enhanced Raman spectroscopy. In addition, I explore how structural parameters such as spacing, symmetry, and lattice arrangement influence the optical response. The broader aim is to bridge fundamental plasmonics with application driven platforms that can be scaled over large areas.

Another important direction in this work is the translation of these metasurfaces toward practical sensing environments. This includes investigating signal uniformity, robustness, and integration with optical setups. By combining materials design with optical characterization, I aim to develop platforms that are both scientifically interesting and technologically relevant.

Key directions:

  • Colloidal plasmonic superlattices and metasurfaces
  • Surface enhanced Raman spectroscopy
  • Hot spot engineering and collective plasmon modes
  • Scalable nanophotonic interfaces

Hybrid Photonic Plasmonic Systems

A major part of my work involves coupling plasmonic nanostructures with photonic platforms to access hybrid optical modes. These systems combine the strong field confinement of plasmonics with the spectral selectivity of photonic resonances. I have explored architectures where metallic nanoparticle assemblies are integrated with dielectric waveguides or periodic optical structures. This leads to the formation of hybrid plasmon photon modes that exhibit enhanced interaction with light. Such coupling enables sharper spectral features and improved sensitivity in optical measurements. I also investigate how angle dependent excitation influences these modes, particularly in the context of spectroscopic techniques. The interplay between geometry, material choice, and optical excitation conditions plays a key role in determining the final response. This line of research is closely connected to the development of next generation nanophotonic devices.

Beyond fundamental understanding, I am interested in translating these hybrid systems toward practical applications. This includes sensing platforms, optical filters, and devices where controlled light matter interaction is essential. The goal is to create systems where design and function are tightly integrated.

Key directions:

  • Guided mode resonance coupled plasmonic systems
  • Hybrid plasmon photon modes
  • Waveguide integrated nanostructures
  • Angle resolved optical spectroscopy

Chiral Nanophotonics

My work in chiral nanophotonics originates from my doctoral research at IIT Delhi, where I developed interference lithography based approaches for the fabrication of three dimensional chiral photonic architectures. These systems were designed to control polarization dependent optical responses such as circular dichroism. By carefully controlling phase relationships in multi beam interference, it was possible to realize complex geometries in a single exposure step. This provided a scalable pathway toward structures that are otherwise difficult to fabricate using conventional methods. The focus during this phase was on understanding how geometry and symmetry breaking influence optical behavior.

More recently, in collaboration with ShanghaiTech University, we have extended these ideas toward colloidal and plasmonic systems. In this work, we demonstrated the self assembly of gold nanospheres into helical architectures using glycopeptide nanofibers as templates. The assembly process is driven by electrostatic interactions and can be tuned by adjusting the pH of the system. This allows controlled transformation of the morphology from short fragments to well defined helical structures. As a result, the chiroptical response can be modulated in a systematic and reversible manner across a broad pH range.

We further investigated how these structural variations influence electromagnetic field distribution using numerical simulations. The results showed that changes in the helical geometry directly impact field propagation and localization, which governs the observed chiral optical response. This establishes a clear link between structure and optical function. The ability to tune morphology and response in situ makes these systems promising for sensing applications, particularly in environments where external stimuli play a role.

Key directions:

  • Interference lithography for chiral nanostructures
  • Chiral plasmonic and photonic metasurfaces
  • Circular dichroism and polarization control
  • Stimuli responsive chiral assemblies

Surface Enhanced Spectroscopy

Surface enhanced Raman spectroscopy forms a central part of my research, particularly in the context of engineered plasmonic and hybrid metasurfaces. A key objective is to understand how nanoscale organization, collective optical modes, and structural symmetry influence electromagnetic field localization and Raman enhancement. In this direction, I have explored template assisted colloidal self assembly strategies to fabricate large area plasmonic architectures with controlled interparticle coupling. By utilizing wrinkle based templates, it is possible to guide nanoparticles into one dimensional and two dimensional arrangements that support dense hot spot distributions. In particular, zigzag wrinkle structures enable the formation of plasmonic nanoparticle chains with enhanced coupling and more isotropic optical response compared to linear assemblies. This provides a route toward uniform and efficient SERS substrates that can be tuned through external parameters such as pH when responsive coatings are incorporated.

Building on these concepts, I further investigate hybrid plasmonic photonic metasurfaces where nanoparticle assemblies are coupled to underlying dielectric modes. These systems enable spectrally selective field enhancement through the formation of hybrid plasmon photon resonances. Using angle resolved SERS, I probe the dispersion of these modes under realistic excitation conditions, including high numerical aperture and oblique incidence. This approach provides direct insight into how guided mode resonances interact with collective plasmonic modes in assembled nanoparticle lattices. A key finding is that the SERS response can be strongly enhanced when the hybrid resonance is tuned to the excitation wavelength, leading to significant intensity increases compared to off resonant conditions.

An important aspect of this work is the robustness of these metasurfaces. By systematically varying particle concentration and structural periodicity, I establish how disorder and lattice variations influence the emergence of hybrid modes. This allows identification of optimal design conditions where enhancement is maximized while maintaining tolerance to fabrication imperfections. The results provide practical design principles for scalable SERS platforms based on colloidal assembly, where both plasmonic coupling and photonic resonance engineering are combined in a single system.

Key directions:

  • Wrinkle templated nanoparticle assembly and zigzag plasmonic chains
  • Hybrid plasmon photonic metasurfaces for SERS
  • Angle resolved spectroscopy and mode dispersion analysis
  • Robust and scalable sensing platforms

Functional Nano Interfaces

A central theme across my research is the design of functional nano interfaces where structural geometry, material composition, and optical or electromechanical response are tightly integrated. These interfaces are not treated as passive platforms, but as active systems in which light matter interaction and signal generation can be engineered at the nanoscale. My work in this direction combines bottom up assembly, soft lithography, and interference based patterning to create scalable architectures that retain functionality over large areas. A key focus is to bridge fundamental nanophotonic design with practical device level implementations, where optical, electrical, and mechanical responses can be coupled within a single platform.

One important direction involves metasurface integrated triboelectric nanogenerators for multifunctional sensing applications. By introducing nanostructured dielectric metasurfaces on flexible polymer films such as PDMS, it is possible to significantly enhance electromechanical coupling and charge generation. Using laser interference lithography and soft molding, large area nanocone metasurfaces can be fabricated with controlled geometry, which increases the effective surface area and boosts triboelectric performance. These metasurface enabled TENG platforms function simultaneously as tactile, proximity, and acoustic sensors within a single ultrathin device. When integrated into array configurations, they allow real time pressure mapping with low crosstalk, while also providing frequency resolved acoustic sensing across a broad spectral range. In addition, the optical diffraction signatures from the metasurface offer an extra degree of functionality for strain and deformation monitoring. This combination of electromechanical and optical responses enables self powered, multimodal sensing platforms for wearable technologies and human machine interfaces.

In parallel, I have developed colloidal quantum dot metasurfaces as scalable platforms for controlling light emission and photonic functionality. Using template assisted self assembly and soft lithographic printing, it is possible to organize perovskite and core shell nanocrystals into periodic structures that act as optical metasurfaces. These systems enable strong control over emission directionality, transforming the inherently omnidirectional radiation of quantum dots into highly directional output. By engineering the periodicity and structural geometry, large area metasurfaces can be fabricated that exhibit significant enhancement in directional emission, as confirmed through angle resolved spectroscopy. Beyond passive emission control, these architectures also support active photonic functionalities such as distributed feedback lasing, where the patterned quantum dot layers act as gain media with tunable resonance conditions and reduced lasing thresholds. Furthermore, by integrating thin metallic layers, hybrid plasmonic modes can be introduced to further tailor emission pathways and enhance radiative coupling into specific directions. This approach enables precise control over optical modes while maintaining low loss and fabrication scalability, making these systems highly relevant for integrated photonic devices, sensing platforms, and light emitting technologies.

Key directions:

  • Metasurface enabled triboelectric nanogenerators and e skin platforms
  • Multimodal sensing combining tactile, acoustic, and optical responses
  • Colloidal quantum dot metasurfaces for directional emission control
  • Distributed feedback lasing and hybrid plasmonic emission engineering
